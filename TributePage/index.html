Augusta Ada King

Countess of Lovelace and the first computer programmer


Here's a time line of Ada Lovelace's life:


  • 1815: Born in London, England.
  • 1824: Her father died when she was eight years old.
  • 1829: She was paralysed after a bout of measles.
  • 1832 She was seventeen, and her mathematical abilities began to emerge, and her interest in mathematics dominated the majority of her adult life.
  • 1833: She was introduced to Charles Babbage.
  • 1835: Married William King-Noel, Lord King, becoming Lady King.
  • 1836: Their first child was born, Byron King-Noel.
  • 1837: Their second child was born, Anne Isabella Noel Blunt.
  • 1838: Ada became Lady Lovelace, Countess of Lovelace.
  • 1839: Their third child was born, Ralph King-Milbanke.
  • 1840: Ada flirted with scandals: firstly from a relaxed relationship with men who were not her husband, which led to rumours of affairs, and secondly, her love of gambling.
  • 1842: Lovelace translated the Italian mathematician Luigi Menabrea's article on Babbage's newest proposed machine, the Analytical Engine.
  • 1844: She commented to a friend Woronzow Greig about her desire to create a mathematical model for how the brain gives rise to thoughts and nerves to feelings ("a calculus of the nervous system").
  • 1852: Died in London, England, from uterine cancer.
  • 1856: Her mother became Baroness Wentworth.
  • 1953: More than a century after her death, Lovelace's notes on Babbage's Analytical Engine were republished. The engine has now been recognised as an early model for a computer and her notes as a description of a computer and software.
